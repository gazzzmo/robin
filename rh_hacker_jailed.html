Computer Age 3

Melbourne Hacker jailed for
obtaining goods by deception
By ROBIN HOWELLS

A computer hacker who used the noms de plumes Bit Mapper, C.C. and Captain Credit on the hackers' bulletin board was sent to jail last Friday.

   Christopher William McDonald, 19, of Arabin Street, Keilor, was sentenced to 12 months jail in the Melbourne Magistrate's Court.

   He was convicted of dishonestly obtaining by deception a computer from Computerscore.

   He was ordered to serve three months of the sentence immediately, with nine months suspended.

   McDonald pleaded guilty to 20 charges of obtaining property by deception and attempting to obtain property by deception.

   He also pleaded guilty to three charges of using the telephone to menace and harass police officers.

   The court was told by the police prosecutor, Sergeant Mick Berkley, that McDonald had ordered $18,705 worth of goods by using credit card numbers belonging to other people.

   The offences took place between 1 July and 17 September last year.

   Sergeant Berkley said that McDonald and two other men, who still had to appear in court, had obtained valid credit card numbers from various sources.

   He gave details of the array of goods McDonald illegally obtained from suppliers.

   This included electronic equipment from Dick Smith Electronics in Sydney, almost 300 tickets from the BASS agency for concerts, including Simply Red and Billy Idol, limousine hire and flowers.

    Details were also given about McDonald's appearance on the 'Willesee' program, recorded on 19 June last year.

   He had been shown contacting a United States hacker by phone to check details of credit cards.

   Sergeant Berkley said that McDonald had obtained the silent home phone numbers and addresses of three detectives involved in handling the case against him and two other men.

   The court was also told that the defendant believed that he could not be caught because the police did not have the facilities to apprehend him.

   Mr Michael Rush, defending McDonald, said that one could see a sophistication in this case "but a great deal of immaturity". He said his client left school at Form 4 at St Bernard's Essendon.

   He first became involved with computers at Assumption College, Kilmore.

   "The hobby has become a fixation for him," he said.

   He said he had been instructed that the co-accused in the case had been granted an adjournment and was going to plead guilty.

   The court was told that McDonald had been listening to the co-accused who made threatening phone calls to the three detectives.

   (At the Melbourne Magistrate's Court earlier in the day, John Halkiadakis, also known as "Blue Thunder", was called before Mr John Duggan, chief magistrate, on the same charges as McDonald. His case was set for a relisting on 10 May to allow him time to instruct legal counsel for his defence. Bail was extended until that day.)   

   The defendant's father, Mr J. A. McDonald, a cartage contractor, told the court that his son was the one of the six children who, until recently, had always been the best behaved.

   He said the first problem with him had been when he got a big phone bill as a result of his computer usage.

   They had a row and his son went to live with a 29-year-old computer enthusiast who had been a bad influence on him.

   He said his son was "an addict". He would use computers daily and nightly if he had the time. "He just thought he was smart … He thought he wouldn't get caught."

   The court was told that McDonald had not had any contact with the other co-accused since charges were laid against them.

   It was also told that McDonald's father had been in hospital following a heart attack since his son committed the offences.

   Referring to the "sophistication shown in the offences", Mr Rush said that the bravado and immaturity were such that his client should be seen by a psychiatrist.

   After adjourning the court to consider the case, Mr Rowan McIndoe said that there were 20 offences of financial dishonesty involving nearly $19,000 committed in less than a month. Less than half the proceeds had been recovered.

   "The operation has been sophisticated, if not precocious or arrogant," he said.

   He also referred to the extent of the offences, the range of them and the perceived impudence with which they had been carried out.

   "The sophistication, the deliberateness of them, should be met by a term of imprisonment served right now," he said.

   Mr McIndoe convicted McDonald on the charge of dishonestly obtaining a computer by deception. He was sentenced to 12 months jail, with nine months suspended.

   On the other 19 charges of attempting to obtain property by deception and obtaining property by deception, he was jailed for one month.

   This is to be served concurrently.

   On the three charges of using the telephone to menace and harass a police officer, he was fined $600.

   The magistrate also ordered McDonald to pay restitution to four of his victims.

   To the BASS ticket agency he is to pay $4500, Astra Hire Cars $3551, Presidential Limousines $1200 and Budget Chauffeur Drive $600.

THE AGE, Tuesday 23 February 1988
