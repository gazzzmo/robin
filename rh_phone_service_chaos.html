PHONE SERVICE
'CHAOS' CLAIM

Blackburn exchange subscribers, concerned about poor telephone service during the conversion of the exchange from July, 1983, to June, 1984, had every reason to complain.


by Robin Howells

   Documents released under the Freedom of Information Act (FOI) show that the main reasons for the unreliable phone service over many months were due to factors that took Telecom months to correct.

   They included:
   Insufficient staff at Blackburn when the work began;
   The age of the old equipment;
   Vital maintenance and upgrading not being done before the installation started;
   Staff away being trained to work on the new sophisticated exchange equipment;
   Inadequate monitoring of the exchange performance;

   Communication breakdowns within Telecom as the problem got worse; and
   A critical shortage of technical staff.

   Documents released under FOI show that Telecom were so concerned that in March and April, 1984, the memos were flying thick and fast trying to stop the stream of consumer complaints.

  'Telecom claim that the ARE-11 exchange now operating at Blackburn requires less maintenance and will provide a more efficient service.

  'It also enables access to ISD with the availability of the Call Charge Record facility.'

   Claims and counter-claims were being made between Telecom's State Support Service, the supervising engineer, Metro East, and the district Telecom manager, Box Hill, about the reasons for the lack of a reliable phone service.

   In a memo to the district Telecom manager, Box Hill, the commercial manager, Mr Keith Bell, wrote on March 4, 1984: "The situation has now reached an intolerable position from both a customer and district/customer services viewpoint.

   "Many customers are most dissatisfied with the level of service they are receiving and want action. Their service has been affected to the extent that they are unable to operate their businesses effectively.

   "Several customers have advised of occasions where they have had to temporarily locate themselves outside the Blackburn exchange area in order to make effective calls."

   He said that the service section of the district office was inundated by calls from irate subscribers and being subjected to constant abuse. He finished by saying that he had frequently discussed the situation with the internal plant manager and the acting district Telecom manager but they had had no success in remedying the position.

   Mr Bell also disclosed that special inspection dockets (three reports to service difficulties in a month) had leapt for Blackburn exchange. For the month ending Febrauary 22, 1984 there were 150 for Blackburn but only 17 for Ringwood.

   On March 6, 1984, in sending Mr Bell's memo to the superintending engineer construction branch, district Telecom manager, Mr Ron Nelson, offered his own view.

   "The service being given to Blackburn exchange subscribers recently has been appalling and many are threatening to carry out an anti-Telecom campaign," he wrote. "Worse than this, many are reporting that they are going out of business, because of their inability to communicate by telephone."

   After detailing technical reasons why there were so many problems at the exchange, Mr Nelson made a startling recommendation.

   Nine months after the conversion had started he wrote: "Because of the high level of unrest among subscribers connected to Blackburn exchange, I suggest we stop all work on ARE-11 conversions until items of common plant are provided in adequate numbers, and until we can ensure at all times a reasonable service will be provided to Blackburn subscribers."

   After analysing all the documents released under the Freedom of Information Act, the Nunawading Gazette is now able to tell readers why they had to put up with such a rotten phone service for nearly a year.

   Telecom's own staff make these comments among more than 50 documents comprising over 260 pages circulated to various departments.

   On March 20, 1984 a Telecom staffer wrote in the work diary kept at the Blackburn exchange: "Although he would not be quoted he believes that insufficient maintenance staff were in the exchange to monitor and fix faults that installers inevitably leave and which appear when installers move on to other areas."

   The same day, the supervising engineer Metro East admitted in a memo that: "Underlying the problem at Blackburn exchange is the failure of the district to bring the equipment to a proper condition before the ARE installation commenced. They were unable to do so because of a critical shortage of technical staff despite many representations..."

   He also said: "A serious situation has developed at Blackburn which should have been advised to us some months ago..."

The Gazette today turns an exclusive spotlight on the telephone service chaos which hit Blackburn subscribers last year.
   Freelance journalist Mr Robin  Howells, of  Nunawading, whose  own  local business was one of the many seriously disrupted during the conversion of Blackburn telephone Exchange to computer operation, decided to investigate.
   He used the Freedom of Information Act to obtain Telecom documents and memos which could reveal the background to the telephone chaos which plagues thousands of local household, industrial and commercial telephone subscribers at the time.
   During his long battle with officialdom on the issue Mr Howells won his appeal to the Administrative Appeals Tribunal over certain documents and papers which Telecom had refused to hand over.
   Mr Howells, 52, is a former news editor of Nation Review, and other publications.

   The assistant superintending engineer - analogue development, Mr A. C. Wright, was quite sure on March 23 why the exchange was in a mess.   

   "It does appear that maintenance staff was barely adequate, if that, to maintain a satisfactory service without the troubles brought on by installation activity. No installation activity is free from fault production and in a big exchange with a large installation staff additional effort should be provided but was not until this week," he wrote. This was nearly nine months after the conversion started.

   Two types of technology, analogue and digital, are used by Telecom to carry speech through the phone network. Analogue exchanges like the ARE-11 one at Blackburn carry signal waves which are continuously varying in shape. Around 90 per cent of the Melbourne suburban network is still on the older analogue technology.

   On March 30, 1984, a network investigation team reported on the investigation of Blackburn congestion following a request by the superintending engineer, field engineering branch.

   It reported: "Maintenance manpower shortage may have contributed to the time lag in fault finding. There was a high level of faults found at Blackburn exchange, of which it is estimated 60 per cent to 70 per cent were installation initiated."

   They recommended additional maintenance staff should be on hand during ARE-11 conversion and specialised equipment, known as an MFC analyser, should be made available for use in fault finding.

   Documents obtained under the FOI Act show that Telecom appeared to be very slow to react to the accumulation of problems.

   On November 29, 1983, the internal plant manager, Box Hill, Mr W. Wilson, wrote to staff at Blackburn, Doncaster and Mitcham exchange saying that district management was concerned about the number of serious equipment failures at exchanges being converted to ARE-11 operation.

  'In many of the Telecom documents quoted here the names of Telecom staff were deleted under the FOI Act. This privacy provision is made where it could reasonably be expected to have a substantial adverse effect on the staff management interests of Telecom if the names were disclosed.'

   In a circular to Box Hill district staff on December 15, 1983, district Telecom manager, Mr Tom Parker, admitted the conversion was not going smoothly. After discussing the low standard of phone service he made a very frank admission.

   "We have had up to 450 reports in one day on the Blackburn exchange, and an increase in the general complaints brought to the attention of all levels within the district."

   A hand-written document dated December 16, 1983, headed "Report on Blackburn exchange excessive fault rate" contains a damning disclosure.

   "The work specs are being performed on relay sets by technical assistants with no experience, and testing after putting relay sets back in service is not being carried out."

   The writer also discloses that ARE-11 training for the officer in charge of the exchange would not be completed until late January and that staff morale was low. This was written six months after conversion started.

   In the year it took to convert the Blackburn exchange to ARE-11 equipment 4123 business and 12,340 private subscribers had to tolerate an unreliable phone service.

   Doncaster exchange is scheduled to be converted to ARE-11 in July, 1985, East Doncaster in May, 1986, and Templestowe in April/June, 1987. In February-June, 1985, Ringwood exchange is being converted from ARF to AXE equipment and Hawthorn from April-June 1985.

   Following the faults that caused so many problems on the Blackburn exchange it is to be hoped that Telecom have learnt a number of valuable lessons from the Blackburn bungling. It was made at considerable cost and inconvenience to their customers.

Nunawading Gazette, Wednesday, April 17, 1985

Author's Note: When the Blackburn phone exchange upgrade was carried out, local people were complaining loud and long about the problems they were experiencing with their phone service. It appeared obvious to me that Telecom Australia was not telling everything they knew about the extent of the problems their customers were reporting.

When I attempted to use the Freedom of Information (FoI) laws to unearth the truth about this fiasco, Telecom fought me bitterly to avoid releasing many of the documents. The Australian Journalists’ Association backed me in my fight with Telecom for all the documents I was seeking.

After getting access to the documents I sought, I made a detailed submission to a Senate Select Committee which was set up to inquiry into the workings of the Freedom of Information Act. I was highly critical of Telecom for the manner in which they attempted to deny documents to me as I was seeking them as a journalist "acting in the public interest". Following my submission, I was invited to appear before the committee at a public hearing in Melbourne.

I wrote a similar article, based on the same FoI documents, for CHOICE, the monthly magazine of the Australian Consumers’ Association. It appeared in the February 1986 issue on pages 34 and 35 under the heading "Telecom bungles upgrading project".
