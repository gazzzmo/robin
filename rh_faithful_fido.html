BBS FILE l ROBIN HOWELLS

Faithful Fido Net

Log on to the National Bulletin Board and you will find it's the Australian gateway to the world's first bulletin board network, known as Fido Net.

   Earlier this year, the National became the first Australian BBS to join the international FidoNet system.

   "Users are able to communicate with others from the US, Europe and UK, Indonesia and the Philippines, as well as all over Australia." says the National's sysop, John Blackett-Smith.

   The FidoNet system is more than two years old, and has grown from an initial system of seven BBS members to an international network with a membership of more than 600. Most of this growth has been in the United States, but the heaviest use is in the Netherlands, where there is a large, active (and highly profitable) club running FidoNet. A smaller FidoNet runs in the UK and a couple of boards are linked in the Philippines and Indonesia.

   The FidoNet system was written by Tom Jennings in America with a little help from some friends. In two and a half years he is already up to Version 11S. According to Blackett-Smith, FidoNet is two modules operating within the same program. The first is a local BBS, not unlike a number of those operating on various IBM PCs and compatibles, such as the Capitol IBBS or the Software Systems IBBS created by Gene Plantz (as used by PC Connection in Melbourne and the PC User Group in Sydney). Along with that is the FidoNet module, which allows a user on the National to leave a message for another user on, for example, a BBS in Ohio.

   At 3 am New York time (6 pm in Melbourne), all the FidoNet systems around the world close down and start dialling each other. Occasionally they will receive an engaged signal and they skip to the next number. Using a complex algorithm developed by Jennings, they all eventually synchronise, and the entire operation takes about seven minutes.

   Once synchronised, all messages are passed on to the other boards, and each board decodes its messages and adds them to its own message section

just as if it had been entered by a local user. Apart from messages, FidoNet also transfers files and data for a newsletter that is circulated to all FidoNet sysops. Blackett-Smith claims that FidoNet is also being used in America by companies which have a need for a network but do not wish to go to the expense of installing a local area or remote network.

   Following on from his role as the sysop for the National, Blackett-Smith is regional co-ordinator for FidoNet and is known officially as "FIDO 1/1" in Australia. Because it is a public domain program, it is free for non commercial use, provided no support is required. A nominal charge of $20 ensures support and upgrades. Charges for commercial users start at $100, and depend upon the level of support required.

   The National BBS is run on an IBM PC compatible, known as the Mac XT, which is imported by KCM Computers in Victoria. It has 640K, two 360K disk drives, and NEC 10M hard disk, and a NetComm In/Modem is used to communicate with callers. Blackett-Smith started the National in August 1985 because he believes that those who are going to succeed in the future are the people who have an understanding of, and some control over, communications.

   "It seems that a lot of people are technology-shy, or afraid of the computer. One way of overcoming this and encouraging further use of data communications is a BBS, particularly if a number of them have a similar user interface," he says. "People need to be comfortable shifting from one to the other, and that will further encourage the dissemination of information."

   He rates the National as being an outstanding BBS success, averaging between 40 and 60 calls per day, despite the fact that it originally closed down between 9 am and 5 pm Monday to Friday so that he could use the line for his film and television production business.

   However, Blackett-Smith warns that only dedicated sysops should consider installing FidoNet, as he estimates that he has spent about 300 hours in getting his system up and running, and fine-tuned into the BBS it is today.
