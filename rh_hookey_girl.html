The girl who played hookey

By ROBIN HOWELLS


Some parents will probably be surprised to hear that when I found out that my daughter Danielle had wagged school one day last week, I was at first surprised at the news, then delighted at her initiative.

   Dan, who hates to be called Danielle, is almost 17, and my wife and I have always encouraged her to be independent. She buys her own clothes, organises her own banking and, like most teenagers, tends to talk for hours on the phone. She likes Roy Harper, 3RRR, Redgum, Virginia Ferguson, Jimi Hendrix, The Beatles and The Minstrel. She wants to buy a new guitar. She is left handed. 'Like Hendrix, Dad'.

   The first I knew that she had wagged school was when a friend phoned me at my office and said that he had seen Dan going down Bourke Street near Russell Street carrying a shopping bag from Gaslight Records. Because she had been spotted around 11 am in the city, when she should have been at school in Croydon, it was obvious that Dan had moved across town with determination.

   That morning she was dropped, as usual, at Mitcham railway station to travel to Croydon and school. I later found out that she crossed the line, bought a return ticket to Melbourne and hit out for the Golden Mile and a day in the record shops.

   The object of all this effort was to buy an LP record by Roy Harper called 'One of Those Days In England', which Dan had only spotted at Gaslight Records. Harper is not on the Top Ten, Twenty, Forty or Hundred but she likes him and now has about eight of about 13 available LPs.

   According to a rock music reference Harper is a 40-year-old Manchester-born megalomanic rock-singing guitarist with a distinctive guitar style. Wrinklies like me will probably be fascinated to know that he is one rock musician who openly admits to having received ECT treatment. That happened after he faked mental illness to bring

about his release from the RAF. Roy Harper became the new magnificent obsession when Dan had collected almost every record ever released by Jimi Hendrix.   

When she was met at Mitcham Station in the afternoon Dan arrived from Croydon as if she had been to school, but we couldn't help noticing the record bag from Gaslight inadequately hidden in her schoolbag.

   As soon as she arrived home she went straight to her room and started playing her latest Roy Harper record. Asked where she got it, she quickly admitted that she had wagged school so that she could travel into the city to buy it.

   Dan was quite proud of herself as she told us how she travelled and did everything for herself, including finding her way from Flinders Street Station to the top of Bourke Street and Gaslight Records. 'It's the first time I've ever made all the arrangements and gone somewhere all on my own', she said.

   After buying the record she went around to Missing Link Records in Flinders Lane and listened to records and checked the stock bins. Having been given a couple of Jimi Hendrix posters at Missing Link, Dan went back to Flinders Street Station and caught the train to Croydon and waited for her school friends to join her on the journey back to Mitcham.

   If it wasn't for my friend spotting her in Bourke Street, I might not have known the full story. Dan was assisted in wagging school by a number of kindly people. Thanks are due to the VicRail porters and guards, the folks at Missing Link and Gaslight record shops, and most of all to the kind man who was a bit early for his appointment with his city dentist last week.

   He told Dan he was early, and he pushed her wheelchair as she trundled up the Bourke Street hill from Russell Street.

Originally published in THE AGE, April 10, 1981
Reprinted in The Best of THE AGE 1980-81

 

Author's Note: Our daughter's name is really Sam, not Dan or Danielle. The name was changed to hide her true identity. When this article was submitted to The Age, I did it using a nom-de-plume. After they published it, I had to admit that I was the author.
