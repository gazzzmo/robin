Must my
mate
leave?



By R. F. HOWELLS

When I called in to Mick's shoe repair shop in Rathdowne Street, North Carlton, last week with a pair of shoes to be soled and heeled, I wasn't prepared for the shock news. After 50 years in Rathdowne Street repairing shoes, Mick Tallon has to close on 24 December. The property has been sold.

   The two-income trendies and property developers who are quickly taking over the Carlton I grew up in probably won't know or miss Mick and his son Frank, who works with him.

   Mick's shop at 641 Rathdowne Street is a solid two-storey 19th century brick building, probably built during the land boom. The corrugated iron verandah, which is in need of repair, is supported by those solid old iron pillars that the sacked city councillors detested. The window needs a clean but it still allows Mick and Frank to wave to friends as they walk past.

   Once you step inside you're welcomed by the smell of leathers, rubber and glue. Shelves and benches are all cluttered and piled high with shoes and boots in various stages of repair. A faded sign on one wall advises that people over 80 accompanied by their parents will be given credit. On another wall is a 1977 calendar embossed on a large rectangle of leather.

   If you happen to arrive near lunch time you'll be tempted by the aroma of minestrone, beans or some other simple dish that Mick is warming for a quick meal. People call in during the day just for a chat. You're not just a number on a docket when you get your shoes repaired at 641, in fact Mick doesn't believe in dockets. He writes your name in a thick pencil on a convenient part of the shoe. Fool proof and personalised.

   Mick Tallon was born in Italy in 1906 and started to learn the craft from a village boot maker when he was five years old. His mother had to pay the boot maker to teach him.

   "It wasn't very long when he told my mother: 'Look, you don't have to pay me any more. I'll give something to the boy.' I was terrible happy," Mick said.

   He migrated to Australia in 1923. Following a stint managing a farm at Shoreham Mick got sick of the country and came to live in Melbourne during the Depression. A cousin who was a shoe repairer in Gertrude Street, Fitzroy, encouraged Mick to make a start in shoe repairing in Carlton and he soon found a shop in Rathdowne Street.

   Mick's area is a retail retreat now with a newsagent, a record shop, the Valrosa Cellars licensed grocer and a great French butcher called La Vache Qui Rit (The Laughing Cow) within the block. He remembers how, before World War II, it was a thriving shopping centre with three fruit shops, three butchers, two delicatessens, two lolly shops and a jeweller.

   "As time went on the Australian people moved out of here, the migrants came with different eating customs and the number of shops declined.

   "Taylor's the delicatessen (we used to call them ham and beef shops then) used to employ five to seven people in the busy period. As the migrants came in all those places died out and businesses and offices took their place."

   Mick is still hoping that a similar fate does not await him.

   As he begins to talk about shoe fashions and quality, Mick's eyes light up and he starts to reminisce.

   "When I started here you could buy a good pair of men's shoes for 7/6 or 75 cents a pair. A very good quality pair of Marshall or Wallace shoes were 21/- or $2.10 a pair. They were first class box calf or kid."

   He remembers Charlie Ward, who conducted community singing in Carlton in the 1930s, complaining about some shoddy shoes at the time. "If Charlie Ward was to come in today and see the rubbish shoes which come into Australia I think his hair would stand on end."

   The army of customers who travel in from places like Caulfield, Oakleigh, Nunawading, Ringwood, Kinglake and Wycheproof are in for a shock if they see the notice in his window about his imminent closure.   

   Mick doesn't want to move away from his beloved Carlton because, as he says, his customers don't have to come to see him too often. 

   "I do use good materials, so that it will last a long time," he said. "Because I have such a lot of customers I want my repairs to last as long as possible so that they don't have to come too often. Not because I don't want to see them - but I want them to get a good wear for their money."

   He used to be next door, at 643 Rathdowne Street, for 41 years and moved into 641 on a two-year lease with yearly renewals. That's when things started to go wrong.

   About two months ago the owner of his premises came and said he had to raise the rent because he had to borrow money on the property at a higher interest rate.

   "I was happy enough," Mick said. "But he didn't say a word that he had the property on the market to sell. It came as a bombshell when he came and said a young fellow had bought the premises.

   "I said to him that I wished I had known before, because there was an empty shop only two doors away that I could have got into. It had been empty a long time."

   He is still looking for a suitable shop around Rathdowne Street, North Carlton.

   "Unless I can find something around here, I'll be stuck," he told me. "I'll have to take all my leather, tools and machinery home until I can get something somewhere."

   Just as I was about to leave, with my skilfully repaired shoes that Mick has kept in good condition for more than seven years, I asked him what his son, Frank, who is in his mid forties, will do if they have to shut up shop completely on Christmas Eve.

   "Frank will have to go on the Government payroll," he said with a kindly twinkle in his eye. "Because he can't do anything else."
