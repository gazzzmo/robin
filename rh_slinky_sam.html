...but slinky Sam makes some stabs anyhow

ISNT it v terrible the way people snub you because you're honest? After I wrote about boring old Borsalino and co a few weeks ago I havent exactly been swept off my feet with invites to previews and premieres. I gather some people didnt like my frankness. Although Philip Nelson, that nice radio personality from the ABC, wrote me a super note in which he expressed a keen desire to meet me tete-a-tete.

   Which is all a v roundabout way of saying I didnt get an invite to the premiere of The great Macarthy last week at Melbourne's marvellous old Athenaeum theatre in Collins street. I'm sure those young whippersnappers who read trendy Nation review wouldnt remember when the Athenaeum had its very own orchestra which used to play before each performance, also at interval.

   Memories of those fine old players came flooding back as I waltzed up Collins street to observe the antics of the bourgeoisie attending the premiere of The great Macarthy. For there, cluttering up the footpath admirably, and adding to the hilarious confusion, were 17 splendid male members (pardon the expression) of the South Melbourne City Band.

   Must I say I was a mite miffed to see that the red carpet which had been run out from the theatre for the beautiful people to walk on was about a foot short of reaching the kerb.

   Standing listening to the band I thought it was simply splendid the way a member of the working class had parked his battered green Ford Zephyr utility smackbang in front of the theatre.

   Noticing a ubiquitous Yellow cab pulling up near the moth-eaten red carpet I held my breath until the occupants appeared. Bliss. Another star for my social constellation. It was none other than John Jarratt, who plays the great Macarthy. He had a very sweet young person with him. Didnt notice what she was wearing because I was all eyes for John who had very smartly flared black slacks, an almost seethrough white shirt with the most exquisite carmine colored embroidery. He also appeared to be wearing an interesting eye shadow and eye liner. Or maybe the job of driving cabs in Sydney between films is giving him "character" lines so young. Poor darling!

   Next I became aware of a very handsome young couple striding down the Collins street hill. It was none other than the Labor member for Casey, Charles Race Thorson Mathews, and his lovely wife Iola. Race, as he prefers to be called (no wonder), was wearing a very serviceable blue suit. However, I think Iola should suggest to him that if he believes, as others do, that he's future PM material he shouldnt wear a brown overcoat with it.

   That wellknown director for ABC television, Christopher Muir, was the next multimedia personality that I spotted. Melbourne readers probably remember Christopher's association with St Martin's theatre about two years ago. Christopher was wearing a v dramatic black alpaca coat. This contrasted magnificently with his gold rimmed glasses and his close cropped blond hair.

   Bruce Spence, of Stork fame, arrived looking as though he hadnt had time to change from a Pram Factory production which investigated the dialectic position of garbage men in society.

   Jack Clancy, who is Nation review's answer to the loved film critic of the Age, Colin Bennett, arrived looking as if he had slept the whole day at Latrobe university in the clothes he was wearing and really looked better up on the screen in the scene in the classroom where he appears as one of Judy Morris's students. He was closely followed by a person called Graeme Blundell who my nonU friends tell me is a person who portrays a sex-crazed Australian male with the unlikely name of "Alvin Purple". Little Graeme was accompanied by his wife Kerry Dwyer.

   Then I saw the most amazing sight of the evening. Gosh, I couldnt believe my eyes. A tall, willowy woman wearing a full length multicolored floral gown entered the theatre. But it was what was around her neck that absolutely amazed me. To the casual observer it may have looked like a feather boa but to my suspicious eye it looked unbelievably like shredded Kotex. How daring can one get?

   My mind was distracted by hearing one of the guests refer to the champagne supper after the show which was to be held at the Athenaeum gallery. After deciding to gently gatecrash it, I tottered off to the Australia hotel to have a quiet drink with a few of my dear male friends at the basement woolshed bar. . . v gay.

   Arriving back at the theatre around 11 pm I was able to mingle with the beautiful people and I actually shared a lift with that well known multimedia personality Vi Greenandahalf who appears on GTV9 with Vi's pad and also writes for the radical Sunday observer. Vi looked wunderbar in a bountiful black tent dress trimmed with a v fetching red flower. For one fleeting moment I thought Wirth's circus was back in town.

   As he got into the lift a rather nice young man said: "How many does this lift hold?" Before anyone else could reply, virago Vi said: "You'd better count me as two." Isnt she absolutely marvellous? What a delicious sense of humor.

   Then I was up at the gallery and fortunately no one was checking invitations. The gallery looks rather like those dreary country mechanics institutes that were the venue for the bachelors and spinsters dances of my dear departed youth.

   One of the stars of The great Macarthy, Judy Morris was absolutely surrounded by a stand of very butch men who were redolent of eucalyptus oil, goanna ointment and sweaty jockstraps. Heavenly.

   I wasnt able to look Judy in the face after remembering what that terribly rude person John Hepworth wrote about her mouth last week. After all, you can't blame the poor girl if she has such a generous mouth, can you?

   I just know all you beautiful people out there have been waiting for Samantha's Top Ten so here it is. I must emphasise that they werent all seen at The great Macarthy.

  1. Bandmaster of South Melbourne City Band
  2. Vi Greenandahalf
  3. Ranald Macdonald, managing director of the Age
  4. Philip Nelson (barefoot)
  5. Phillip Adams
  6. Philip the greek
  7. Kevin Arnett on skis
  8. Barbara Walker, wife of Melbourne's lord mayor Ronald
  9. Iola Mathews
  10. Judy Morris

Nation Review - August 15-21, 1976
