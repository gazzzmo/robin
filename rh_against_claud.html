CLAUD WOODS is a name that's hard to forget. In the early 1970s Claud was actively associating with the National Socialist party of Australia, a minuscule group more commonly called nazis.

   Imagine my concern to see that late last month the Melbourne Herald reported that Claud Woods, the treasurer of People Against Communism, and Alain Birtley, PAC vice president, were chased and beaten by a group of demonstrators.

   Woods was fortunate enough to escape from the mob with bruises but Birtley had to be hospitalised with a punctured lung, broken ribs and bruises. Police have arrested a man and charged him with assault and assault with a weapon.

   After the attack the president of People Against Communism, Jennifer McCallum, said that she was more determined than ever to get maoists and trotskyites outlawed. She also said the PAC was trying to raise enough money to cover Birtley's hospital and medical costs.

   This week I phoned Ms McCallum to ask if she was aware that Claud Woods had been a member of the National Socialist party of Australia and had been the executive officer of the central executive.

   With her frank forthrightness Ms McCallum said: "He's no more a nazi than I am. He's the most hardworking member we've got."

   Because the police had said that some of the demonstrators outside the Hilton were wearing nazi swastika armbands, I asked if she knew if Woods and any of his PAC companions were adorned in this way. "Good God no. The police can vouch for that," she replied.

   She did say that Claud has a very quick temper but she was also quick to point out that she thought he was no more a fascist than she was. Ms McCallum also said that PAC's legal adviser, sir Reginald Sholl had said that if anything is printed about Woods by any paper they'll be sued.

   Referring to Woods' activities with the NSPA during the Viet Nam moratorium marches she said: "I admire Claud Woods for the stand he took during the moratoriums." But her piece de resistance came when she disclosed that Woods is a member of a lodge and a mason.

   When he was asked if he had told PAC of his previous association with a leading NSPA person and his involvement as the executive officer of the NSPA central executive, Woods denied that he was ever a member or a card holder of the NSPA.

   Although I referred him to the mention he gets in a letter NSPA member Katrina Young wrote to fellow member Dr E. R. Cawthron (reported in David Harcourt's book Everyone wants to be fuehrer) Woods continued to deny membership of NSPA.

   In a further effort to jog Woods' memory I reminded him of the Melbourne Herald report of the attempt by eight nazi party members to distribute pamphlets in the Melbourne city square during the may day celebrations.

   The Herald said: "Claud Woods of the nazi party was hit in the face by a man in the city square today. Mr Woods lost a tooth and got a cut lip. Several people bustled two of the members in party uniform and police intervened. A group of students tried to burn a swastika armband but police took it from them. Soon afterwards police had to manhandle several people away from the nazis as tempers flared."

   Woods said that although he may have been standing near the nazis in the city square that day it didnt make him a member. He was willing to admit that he had some association with leading australian nazi Cass Young and added as an aside: "The guy was also jewish, by the way."

   At that stage it seemed rather pointless to ask Woods if he remembered being photographed as he was being attacked by a Footscray shopkeeper in 1971 when a group of national socialists held a demonstration against communism. Or if he recalled being photographed leading members of the NSPA in a demonstration in Brisbane in 1969 against the Red and Black bookshop. Claud's sign said Free gasoline and matches for NLF and the one being carried behind him said Communism is jewish.

   On wednesday night Ms McCallum announced that PAC has expelled Woods. She also said the executive had been "misled in relation to this matter" but refused to say how.

   Woods was phoned by Ms McCallum on tuesday night (after I had queried her about her knowledge of his earlier involvement with the nazis) and asked for his resignation. On wednesday it was decided to expel him.

   She disclosed that the decision to expel Woods had been made after she talked to one of the PAC patrons and its legal adviser sir Reginald Sholl.

Nation Review - 8-14 october 1976

 

Author's Note 1: People Against Communism was one of a number of strange groups which appeared in Australia in the 1970s. When my attention was drawn to the fact that Claud Woods was a member, I started to wonder if PAC were aware of his interesting background and history of political activism. After asking the questions I did of PAC and Woods, I was advised by friends to be more aware of my personal security.

Years later I discovered that Claud has been employed for many years in a highly influential, and often feared, federal government department!

Author's Note 2: In many documents pertaining to his activities, Claud's name has been misspelt as "Claude Woods". Unconfirmed reports claim this even happened in certain government agency files!
