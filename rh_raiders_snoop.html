A keen group of young computer 'hackers' could be spying
on your confidential memos as you sleep

Every night while business sleeps, critical corporate records are being revealed to outsiders with the power to pass the information on to competitors.

   A team of 21st century burglars today poses arguably the greatest threat to Australian business. These modern day safecrackers, known as "hackers", are stalking the computer installations of some of Australia's biggest companies with extraordinary ease. In many instances their task is made easier by the general ignorance of business of their power and very existence.

   However, a cross-section of these hackers contacted by Today's Computers claim to have cracked the computers of such corporate giants as BHP, Elders-IXL, BP, Ansett, TNT, ICL and Telecom as well as educational institutions such as the Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology. When many of these companies were contacted they claimed no knowledge of any intrusion and said they thought their systems were secure. However information supplied by hackers allowed Today's Computers staff to easily penetrate supposedly burglar-proof computers.

   It seems there are very few systems, either mainframe or PC-based, that are completely safe. In fact, one of the easiest computers to break into was operated by a leading computer company.

   The prospect of an intruder "touring" the financial accounts of business with the ability to change and even destroy the information is a concerning prospect to say the least.

   Even more frightening are the "virus codes" which have been detonated in the US. These codes electronic time bombs which sit undetected in a computer's memory until a programmed time sets off the device - can completely destroy systems or automatically change data.

   Since the computer age allowed storage of highly sensitive financial and operational data electronically, many computers - mainframes and networks of minis and micros - are at risk because of the sophisticated programming techniques employed by hackers.

   Kevin Fitzgerald, a computer security consultant and executive director of Chisholm Institute of Technology's computer abuse research bureau, says it is only a matter of time before professional criminals move in, luring the young amateurs into the million dollar world of computer fraud and electronic espionage.

   Fitzgerald, who consulted the Costigan inquiry, has details of more than $6.2 million in losses directly attributable to 80 cases of computer abuse with another 70 cases of unknown loss.

   An exclusive survey of a group of the hackers conducted by Today's Computers revealed many stole passwords and phone numbers to dial up computers while on work experience at companies. Others obtained information from employees.

   But investigations have also revealed the existence of a bulletin board, an electronic message service, where the hackers meet to exchange phone numbers, passwords and information on cracking a range of computers both here and overseas.

   One of their favorite visiting spots once they break into a new system is the passwords file. If they can nab some more passwords and secure a higher level of access they rate it as a "top hack".

   Hacking into business computers is just a grand game to many of the hackers. They've given away Dungeons and Dragons and created their own new game. It should be called Bulk Byte Banditry. Their hero is definitely David Lightman, the computer kid of the film, War Games.

   Considering the guidance given on BBSs for the serious hacker, claims about the number of computers they have hacked should be believed. This particular board provides highly detailed information on a number of files which can be called up and studied by the trusted few.

   Determined hackers can start with The Hacker Notebooks - 1, 2, and 3, which might well be subtitled "Everything You Wanted To Know About Hacking - But Were Afraid To Ask Telecom". Reading Hackfile 1 will also add to a hacker's knowledge. So will NUIS (an NUI is a Network User Identifier that enables a computer user to pass through Midas or Austpac to connect to other computers). Midas is Australia's overseas  packet-switching system run by the Overseas Telecommunications Commission. Austpac is Australia's packet-switching network. PSS Gate (how to use), PSS Network (tutorial) and JANET (tutorial) are all there for the reading. The PSS files deal with Packet Switching Systems and enables a hacker to access mainframe computers all over Australia from the safety of his own home. JANET is the name of a British academic network of computers located at universities and polytechnics throughout the country.

   A 17-year-old hacker who hides behind the nom-de-plume The Wizard (not the author of Wizard's Notebook in Today's Computers) is said to have acquired these files by gaining access to Minerva with a cracked ID code. He then dialled up Britain using his modem and got the information from a database. They are general "help" files which a legitimate user would have access to when logged on to a system. Minerva is an internationally compatible electronic mail service to link terminals and computers.

   Four articles by The Knights of the Shadow (a group of hackers who achieved some notoriety in the US) deal with hacking introduction, hacking DECs, hacking VAX and UNIX. This collection of articles gives a very thorough grounding in hacking three of the most common mainframe computers installed in Australia. These articles appeared on the hackers BBS after some of the Australian hackers phones similar BBSs in the US and downloaded the information to place it on the local board.

   Most hackers who answered the survey questions listed the main sources of their information on passwords as being other hackers, closed BBSs, the Minerva Hackers' Conference, downloading from other hackers, and "friends in high places". Some said they had obtained passwords while on work experience, others claimed to have "got them while visiting government departments and talking to programmers".



   Once they have a computer number they dial up using computers costing as little as $300  with a $200 modem to steal their way into million dollar mainframes. Even without exact phone numbers they can use demon dialers - programs that repeatedly dial a random series of numbers until a computer responds. A similar program can be used to bombard computers with a series of passwords generated by dictionary programs until entry is gained, but in many cases passwords are simply guessed - "PASS" for example. The hackers claim to follow a code of ethics, but in practice they often appear to forget the rules.

   Melbourne is the home of Australia's computer hackers. There are two hacking boards in Melbourne and the only other known ones are in Canberra and the Northern Territory. However, because a number of hackers are involved in "phone phreaking" (illegal methods of making free phone calls), distance from a mainframe computer is merely another challenge.

   Sitting in one corner of McDonald's hamburger shop they appear just like any other bunch of teenage youths - but they are decidedly different. This group, and their absent friends, should be feared by many businesses which run mainframe computers. They are the new, young breed of computer hackers meeting for a HACKFEST '86.

   However, although they are depicted as super-smart by the media, they are not quite as clever as many Australian business people have been led to believe. They certainly hack into and pay unauthorised and unwanted visits to a number of business computers but they appear to lack the expertise of British and US hackers.

   With the co-operation of the "sysop" of a leading hacking board, this survey of Australian computer hackers has uncovered some interesting facts. A "sysop" is a system operator who ensures that a bulletin board being run on a computer is functioning in the way the program intends.

   One particular bulletin board is widely recognised among Victorian hackers as being the most prestigious to belong to and contribute to. Most members hide their identity behind names such as Night Stalker, Captain Hack, System Cruncher, The Dwarf, Phanton Phreak, and so on. Most of them live in fear of being apprehended by Telecom's data investigation staff. "Many of them jump into their boxes and hide when they think Telecom is trying to trace them," one of the older, wilier hackers told me.

   Although about 170 computer buffs log onto his BBS as regular callers with a password, fewer than 35 have been granted privileged access to allow them to see hidden sections of the board devoted to hacking. This group within the group were all invited to answer a questionaire about themselves and their hacking achievements and attitudes.

   Asked to estimate the number of computer systems they had hacked into, 35 per cent claimed to have penetrated between 10 and 15. Twenty-eight per cent admitted to two to five penetrations, and 14 per cent claimed five to 10. Seven per cent of the hackers claimed to have "visited" more than 50 computers. Nearly 90 per cent of the hacking is done into mainframes, with the remainder into PCs.

   The Minerva Hackers' Conference, which was held on the Minerva system in November last year, was one of the more daring exploits carried out by a group of Australian hackers.

   "One of the hackers acquired a Minerva ID (identification account) and passed it on to some other users on the board," a participant said. "They all got on about 10 pm and went to the area called Net Talk. This is an area with a conferencing system where multiple users can talk to each other. Then they got more IDs and more user numbers and every night for weeks they would get on to Midas about 10 pm and wait until other hackers came on so they could start chatting. Bits of hacking information were exchanged using the facilities of Midas."

   Business people concerned about the safety and integrity of data on their own computers have good reason to be worried. Hackers in the survey who are working occupy jobs such as computer programmers, computer technicians, computer operators and commercial data processing and technical positions in telecommunications corporations.

   Most hackers expressed disbelief at the simplicity of many of the passwords they have discovered. The most common passwords found were a girlfriend or wife's name, part of a surname or company name, users' initials, the name of the computer system, simple groups of letters or numbers (such as ABCDE or 12345). Common passwords that make a hacker's job easier include such Golden Oldies as BOSS, DEMO, PASS, SEXY, ALPHA, OPEN, GO, VISITOR, MANAGER, FRED and PHONE. If you are using one of these tired old troopers, trade it in quickly on something more original.

   Most passwords used by hackers do not appear to be acquired by skill or ingenuity. They are more often stolen or acquired by other means from offices. Employees either deliberately, or unwittingly, make them known to unauthorised users and they soon appear on hackers' bulletin boards.

   Hackers go to extraordinary lengths to protect their illegally obtained password booty. A recent message on a hacking board indicates their concern:

   "Just watch out what you do on Minerva. If the accounts come in and out as you guys have said, then I suggest you exercise extreme caution. I was traced once from Minerva, after I incorrectly typed in the ID. I switched off quickly though. Just be careful . . . that's how The Knights of the Shadow were caught (I think)."

   Other messages on the same BBS gave surprisingly detailed information on how Telecom can do a phone trace on everyone who logs on to Austpac or Midas.

   One of the cunning tricks used by local hackers is called "the decoy". When they have acquired a low level account on a computer system they aim to upgrade this to a higher level. What they do is to write a program which appears to be the log-on procedure of the computer they have penetrated. This program is written to appear just as the computer system usually does when a registered user logs on. It then prompts the user for an account name and password.    More than 60 per cent of the hackers were aged between 16 and 18, while another 20 per cent were between 22-25. Only six per cent were over 30. The biggest group, 50 per cent, had reached year 11 at school, with the next biggest group being 21 per cent who had reached year 10. Those who had done more than 12 years schooling were next, comprising 14 per cent of the group. Ninety-three per cent of the respondents said they were males.

   It took 12 to 18 months of using computers before 21 per cent attempted to hack into a system, but 14 per cent started hacking after three to six months and another 14 per cent after six to 12 months. In their first three months of computing, seven per cent started hacking into other people's computers. The majority, 42 per cent, didn't start hacking until after 18 months.

   Most of the hackers use very simple home computers. Apple is the most popular, followed closely by the inexpensive Commodore C64 and Tandy's TRS models. The modems used are the cheapest available, costing about $200.

   To search out unlisted phone numbers of business computers most hackers use a simple computer program, written in Basic, called the Demon Dialler. This program allows a hacker to dial a selected group of telephone numbers and record when the carrier tone of a computer modem is detected.

   Hackers refuse to disclose details of the information they read and study when they "visit" business computers. One of them said: "We only share our knowledge with other hackers, not reporters. Why should we supply you with information you want and watch everything dry up."

   A recent message on the board indicates how seriously most hackers try to cover their unauthorised entries into computers. One of the hackers wrote to fellow hackers: "I shouldn't need to remind you that it's far better to stalk a system than to wreck it and hack it openly (p/w's don't last long like that!) If you don't understand . . . when you get into a system act like a spy would and tread softly! And if you lot don't behave we'll have to tighten access to this board, SO BE WARNED!"

   The hacker "code of ethics" consists of three points:

   1. No hacker will ever delete or damage information that belongs to a legitimate user of the system in any way that the hacker cannot easily correct.

   2. No hacker will leave another hacker's name or phone number on any computer system. He will leave his own on a system only at his own risk.

   3. All hackers are expected to obtain and contribute their own account information.

   Most hackers follow these rules most of the time, because: (a) They would like to keep the account information they went to so much trouble to get; (b) They would like to stay out of legal trouble, if possible; (c) They like computers and don't have any reason to cause trouble to them or for the people who run them; (d) They love the elaborate, complex logic of computer systems. People who find pleasure in destroying data are not motivated by love of the system or by respect for other hackers.

Today's Computers, April 1986

Author's Note: In 1985 and 1986 I became increasingly interested in the exploits and diverse skill of a number of computer hackers and phone phreaks I had made friends with in Melbourne, Australia. With the co-operation of the Sysop of a hackers’ bulletin board, I arranged to do a survey aimed at measuring the activities of this group. I was surprised at the amount of information they were willing to disclose. This survey and my own independent observations made on this BBS and others devoted to hacking provided the detail needed for this article.

When the article appeared I chose not to have my name associated with it. For personal reasons, I did not want to bring myself to the attention of Telecom Australia, later to change its name to Telstra Corporation Ltd!
