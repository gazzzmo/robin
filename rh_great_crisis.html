Melbourne
By ROBIN HOWELLS

WHENEVER the next federal elections are called Australia is likely to see a polarisation of the electorate unmatched since those wild meetings of the 1960s when Vietnam and conscription split siblings, saints and sinners.

   That was my main impression after just 15 minutes at the Liberal party rally at the Myer music bowl last sunday afternoon.

   When the contingent of over 200 ALP stirrers came striding down the slippery sloped lawn just as the meeting started you realised that at long last Malcolm Fraser had given them a cause to be genuinely concerned about. Starting with their big banner that said Libs pack rape democracy they made their presence prominent.

   The crowd of around 12,000 was unusual, too. They were almost 100 percent Liberal party supporters. You could identify them by their Liberal party tee-shirts, badges, balloons and stickers. There seemed to be almost no uncommitted members of the public along to hear Fraser.

   Which was just as well, because from the moment Fraser tried to talk it was almost impossible to hear him. The heavy amplification couldnt match the fervour of the ALP stirrers shouting: "One two three four, who do you think we're voting for? Five six seven eight, Fraser must capitulate" and "We want Gough, We want Gough" over and over.

   Posters held by the ALP supporters said Hitler also claimed he had to do it. Save democracy; Gorton, then Snedden. Now he is prepared to stab Australia in the back in search of power; Fraser's stormtroopers threaten our democracy and Malignant Malcolm. Constitution's cancer.

   Then it was a switch to shouting zieg heil and giving Fraser the fascist salute. This riled many Liberal supporters and set off a series of minor scuffles and verbal jousts that kept feelings high through the rest of the rally.

   Among the strongest supporters of the Liberals at the rally were groups with middle european accents who were obviously most concerned by the developing political crisis. They maintained an incredible tirade of insults and abuse aimed at the ALP stirrers.

   Fraser was supported in his attempst to try to indicate a wave of public support for his actions by senator Margaret Guilfoyle, Doug Anthony and his permanent plastic smile, and Rupert Hamer looking rather dowdy in slacks, a dull jumper and a drab sports coat. Athol Guy, that amazing self-Seeking Liberal member of state parliament was responsible for the "musical" entertainment.

   Phillip Lynch, Tony Staley, Peter Nixon, Andrew Peacock and Susan Peahen were also there to keep up the numbers. It was also reported that there were 140 members of the Victoria police in attendance.

   George Cox, the Liberal candidate for the state seat of Deakin, left the meeting while his beloved leader was still trying to explain why he had to do it to revive Australia. By a strange quirk of fate I then went off to a rather interesting film called Paper tiger.

   The next day didnt look like being a dull monday. To show support for the government the ALP had arranged at short notice a rally in the city square at 12.30 pm. By 12.15 the square was filling rapidly and it was notable that the audience wasnt just made up of obvious ALP supporters.

   They came out of shops and offices at lunchtime and they had an air of spontaneity about them. They didnt seem orchestrated and programmed in the way the Liberals did the day before.

   Clyde Holding, Peter Redlich and senator Wheeldon warm the crowd up prior to Gough's appearance. Redlich raises hearty laughter from the square when he says: "The people down near the Fraser stinks sign, can you hear me? And please keep the sign up."

   Other signs refer to Fraser political prostitute and the S in Fraser is a swastika: Mal, multinationals pal; The Spanish workers support Labor government; Stop Mal-practice and 95 percent of Greeks support democratic stand of Australian government.

   It's 12.30 now and the city square is packed with more than 18,000 people. It's become such a crush that the crowd spills out to the middle of Collins street and the police are forced to divert westbound traffic around the block. In Swanston street the crowd is also taking over the road and trams are forced to a halt.

   Gough arrives and is cheered so loud and long that the pigeons on the Melbourne town hall opposite increase their attempts to turn it white. Tickertape showers down on the square and Gough gets into his speech with gusto.

   He makes it plain that Labor will not bow to opposition pressure for a house of representatives election. "Your great response will ensure that government of the people, for the people, by the people's house will not disappear from this country," he says.

   In referring to the recent death of senator Milliner he makes great play of the fact that Fraser is trying to seize power over a dead man's body. Despite patchy rain and temporary trouble with the amplification system during the prime minister's address the crowd stays to hear every word.

   At the end of his address Whitlam is surrounded by wellwishers and he has a hell of a job to get back to his car. The crowd continues to follow his car up Swanston street and Melbourne staggers back to normal.

NATION REVIEW - october 24-30, 1975

Author's Note: Before the dismissal of the Whitlam Labor government by Sir John Kerr on November 11 1975, a wave of demonstrations and protests swept the country. Many Australians were angered by the flouting of convention by members of the Liberal opposition. They were refusing to approve the Budget sent to the Senate by the Labor government. This report was one of a number carried in a double page tabloid spread, which Nation Review ran to cover the reaction taking place.
